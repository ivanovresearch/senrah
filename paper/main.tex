\title{Leak-Free Temporal Evaluation of Precedent Retrieval over
  Merged Pull-Request History}

\author{Vladimir Ivanov}
\affiliation{%
  \institution{Independent Researcher}
  \country{}}
\email{vladimir@ivanovresearch.dev}

\begin{abstract}
Retrieval tools for AI coding agents are trivial to demo and hard to
measure. We study the evaluation of \emph{precedent retrieval} --- ranked
semantic search over a repository's merged pull-request history, where each
item is a problem--solution pair --- and show that the standard known-item
protocol, while necessary, systematically overstates practical utility. On a
backport-deduplicated corpus of 575 merged PRs from \texttt{dotnet/efcore},
known-item retrieval achieves recall@5 = 0.90 (MRR@10 = 0.79, 218 held-out
queries), yet this measures only ranking quality. We construct a leak-free
temporal-holdout instrument for the harder question --- forward coverage:
whether a genuinely new task retrieves a relevant precedent that strictly
predates it. A metadata-linked relevance label left the instrument
underpowered (5 answerable tasks against a pre-registered floor of 80); a
30-task labeled probe attributed the failure to the label, not the corpus,
undercounting true relevance by 14--29$\times$. We rebuild relevance with
TREC-style pooling over three retrieval legs and a blind human gold set of
270 pairs (24\% positive; 29/30 tasks answerable). An LLM judge intended to
scale the labeling failed its pre-registered calibration gate (Cohen's
$\kappa = 0.39$ vs.\ a 0.6 floor; recall 0.35 against human labels,
concentrated exactly on the semantic-transfer cases the pool exists to
capture) and was retained as advisory-only. We report this negative result
as a first-class finding and derive design guidance for evaluating
history-grounded retrieval without laundering coverage numbers through
uncalibrated judges.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{mining software repositories, retrieval evaluation, AI coding
agents, temporal holdout, TREC pooling, LLM-as-judge calibration}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

AI coding agents increasingly operate inside mature codebases whose
conventions, workarounds, and edge-case lore live not in the current source
but in years of merged pull requests. \emph{Precedent retrieval} makes that
history available at write time: each merged PR is indexed as a
problem--solution pair (the issue or description that states what was wrong,
and the diff the team actually accepted), and an agent working on a new task
retrieves the most similar prior precedents. The reference implementation
studied here, \textsc{senrah}, is an open-source, self-hosted retrieval
server that embeds problem and solution texts separately and serves ranked
results to any agent over the Model Context Protocol~\cite{mcp2024}.

This paper is not about the retriever. It is about the measurement --- which
we found to be, by a wide margin, the harder engineering problem. A
retrieval tool is easy to make \emph{look} good: pick a few queries where it
returns something plausible, screenshot the result, ship. The failure mode
of that approach is that one never learns whether the tool works; one learns
whether one can cherry-pick. We therefore ask two distinct questions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Ranking quality.} When a relevant target is known to exist,
    does it rank near the top? This is the classical known-item
    setup~\cite{sparck1975ideal,voorhees2000variations}.
  \item \textbf{Forward coverage.} For a \emph{new} task, does a
    genuinely-relevant precedent that strictly predates it exist in the
    history, and does the system retrieve it --- with no leakage from the
    future? This is the question that decides whether precedent retrieval
    is actually useful, and it is the one that is easy to get subtly,
    invalidatingly wrong.
\end{enumerate}

Answering the second question forced a sequence of methodological decisions
that we believe generalize beyond our system, and one measured failure that
we believe the community should see reported rather than buried. Our
contributions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item[\textbf{C1.}] A \textbf{leak-free temporal-holdout design} for
    retrieval over PR history, with leakage controls that are checked
    rather than assumed: the corpus/query split freezes on merge timestamps
    and original ingest state (PR bodies are editable after the fact),
    relevance labels freeze together with the corpus boundary, and the
    scorer applies the product's own score threshold so the instrument
    measures what a user actually gets.
  \item[\textbf{C2.}] Evidence that \textbf{metadata-linked relevance labels
    underpower temporal instruments}: under an issue-link label, only 5 of
    278 post-cutoff tasks were answerable against a pre-registered floor of
    80. A 30-task labeled probe attributed the failure to the label (a
    14--29$\times$ undercount of true relevance), not to genuine precedent
    scarcity --- a distinction that a silent pivot back to known-item
    metrics would have hidden.
  \item[\textbf{C3.}] A \textbf{TREC-pooled, human-calibrated relevance
    definition} for PR-history retrieval. A blind gold set of 270
    (task, candidate) pairs, labeled before any system result was
    inspected, validates the three-leg union pool: the production embedding
    leg is precise (0.51), and the low-precision lexical and second-family
    embedding legs still contribute 29\% of all relevant precedents ---
    the classical pooling trade~\cite{zobel1998reliable}.
  \item[\textbf{C4.}] A \textbf{negative result on LLM-as-judge}: two
    frontier models fail a pre-registered calibration gate
    ($\kappa \le 0.39 < 0.6$)~\cite{cohen1960}, with misses concentrated
    precisely on the semantic and convention-transfer cases the pool was
    widened to capture. We keep the judge advisory-only and accept that
    scaling the instrument remains human-labeling-bound.
\end{itemize}

Throughout, we held four principles: pre-register the gate before the
results exist; label blind before you look; treat the first number as a
symptom, not a finding; and report negative results as results, because a
measurement you cannot trust is worse than no measurement.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Known-item evaluation.}
Known-item retrieval --- find the one document known to answer the query ---
is among the oldest evaluation designs in IR~\cite{sparck1975ideal}. It
requires no relevance judging beyond the query--target link, which makes it
cheap and reproducible, but it measures only whether a target the system is
\emph{told} exists can be ranked highly. Voorhees's work on judgment
variation~\cite{voorhees2000variations} and Zobel's analysis of pooling
reliability~\cite{zobel1998reliable} establish the broader point we rely
on: what one measures is determined by how relevance is defined, and
narrow definitions bias conclusions.

\paragraph{Temporal splits and leakage.}
Time-respecting evaluation is standard advice in mining software
repositories, yet leakage enters through subtle channels: retrospectively
edited artifacts, re-ingested corpora that silently move the split
boundary, and labels computed over post-cutoff state. Kalliamvakou et
al.~\cite{kalliamvakou2014promises} catalogue the perils of mining GitHub
data at face value; our design (\S\ref{sec:temporal}) treats their
warnings as checklist items --- e.g., PR descriptions are mutable, so the
split must freeze on merge timestamps and original ingest state, not on
current body content. Benchmarks for repository-level agent tasks such as
SWE-bench~\cite{jimenez2024swebench} face analogous contamination
questions from model training data; our instrument addresses the
retrieval-side analogue.

\paragraph{Pooling.}
Judging every (task, candidate) pair is infeasible at corpus scale. TREC
pooling~\cite{voorhees2000variations,zobel1998reliable} unions the top
candidates of multiple independent systems into a judgment pool; relevance
is then judged only inside the pool. Pool composition is a known source of
bias --- a pool built from one system family under-represents what only
other families retrieve. Our pool (\S\ref{sec:pooling}) therefore unions an
embedding retriever, a lexical BM25 leg~\cite{robertson2009bm25}, and a
second-family embedder (bge-m3~\cite{chen2024bge}), and we validate each
leg's contribution against blind human labels.

\paragraph{LLM-as-judge.}
Scaling relevance labeling with an LLM judge is attractive and increasingly
common~\cite{zheng2023judging}. The known risk is that judge agreement with
humans is task-dependent and must be measured, not assumed. We
pre-registered a Cohen's $\kappa$~\cite{cohen1960} authority gate and
report a failure against it (\S\ref{sec:judge}); the failure mode ---
misses concentrated on convention-transfer relevance --- is, to our
knowledge, underdocumented and directly actionable for others building
retrieval evals over software history.

\paragraph{Context for coding agents.}
Contemporary context tools embed \emph{current} code (semantic code
search~\cite{husain2019codesearchnet}, repository indexing in commercial
assistants). Precedent retrieval is complementary: it embeds how the code
has historically been \emph{changed} --- problem--diff pairs from merged
PRs~\cite{gousios2014exploratory} --- which is precisely the knowledge that
is absent from current source and from base-model training in private,
legacy codebases.

\section{System Under Evaluation}
\label{sec:system}

\textsc{senrah} ingests merged PRs from a repository (resumable traversal;
bot and automation filtering; oversized-PR exclusion), stores problem text
and diff, and embeds them separately with
\texttt{text-embedding-3-small}~\cite{openai2024embeddings} (1536
dimensions, token-aware truncation) into PostgreSQL with
pgvector HNSW indexes~\cite{malkov2020hnsw} on both columns. Retrieval
embeds the query once, oversamples by approximate nearest-neighbor search
on both columns, and re-ranks by a weighted problem/solution cosine
composite; results below a score threshold are withheld and surfaced as a
below-threshold hint rather than returned as matches. In the frozen
evaluation protocol the problem/solution weights are 0.7/0.3. The system is
open source; all evaluation manifests are hash-pinned and runs are
deterministic.

\section{Instrument 1: Known-Item Retrieval}
\label{sec:knownitem}

\paragraph{Definition.}
For a known issue whose fixing PR is known, does that PR appear in the
top-$k$ results when the issue text is used as the query?

\paragraph{Corpus and protocol.}
\texttt{dotnet/efcore}, deduplicated: 575 merged PRs (merged 2024-04-06
through 2026-06-12), 218 held-out queries. The manifest is hash-pinned and
the run is deterministic.

\paragraph{Deduplication is load-bearing.}
A naive corpus double-counts backports and cherry-picks: the ``same'' fix
appears several times, so \emph{a} hit is easy and recall inflates. We
detect backport clusters from stored diffs using union-find over
\emph{corroborated} edges --- diff similarity alone is never allowed to
merge two PRs --- and score \textbf{per-cluster}: a hit on any cluster
member counts once, and distractors are likewise counted per-cluster. The
divergence between per-PR and per-cluster scoring is demonstrated on a
fixture where the two disagree.

\begin{table}[h]
  \caption{Known-item retrieval on deduplicated \texttt{dotnet/efcore}
  (575 merged PRs, 218 held-out queries, per-cluster scoring).}
  \label{tab:knownitem}
  \begin{tabular}{lr}
    \toprule
    Metric & Value \\
    \midrule
    recall@1 & 0.71 \\
    recall@5 & 0.90 \\
    recall@10 & 0.93 \\
    MRR@10 & 0.79 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Why this is not the whole story.}
Known-item recall answers ``can the system rank a target it is told
exists.'' It says nothing about coverage for genuinely new work. Worse, on
a deeper corpus known-item recall can \emph{fall}, because more PRs means
more near-duplicate distractors competing for top-$k$ slots --- so
optimizing this number alone can penalize exactly the corpus growth that
increases real utility. A second instrument is required.

\section{Instrument 2: Leak-Free Temporal Holdout}
\label{sec:temporal}

\paragraph{Design.}
Pick a cutoff time $T$. Everything merged strictly before $T$ is the
retrievable corpus; everything merged strictly after $T$ is the incoming
task stream. For each post-$T$ task: does a relevant precedent exist
strictly before $T$, and does the system retrieve it? This simulates the
deployed setting --- an agent working today, retrieving from history.

\paragraph{Leakage controls (where temporal evals die).}
\begin{itemize}
  \item The corpus filter is strictly $\mathit{merged\_at} < T$; queries
    strictly $\mathit{merged\_at} > T$. The split freezes on
    $\mathit{merged\_at}$ and the \emph{original} ingest timestamps ---
    never on current PR-body state, which is editable after the
    fact~\cite{kalliamvakou2014promises}.
  \item Relevance labels and the corpus boundary freeze together, so a
    later re-ingest cannot silently move the boundary under the labels.
  \item The scorer applies the product's own below-threshold cutoff, so
    the instrument measures what a user actually receives, not an
    idealized ranking.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Materialization.}
The full multi-year history was ingested for this instrument
(\texttt{efcore}: 487 $\rightarrow$ 8{,}449 PRs; one ingest, one index,
every depth rung materializable from it), and a deep cluster map built
(9{,}594 PRs, 397 multi-member backport clusters, hash-pinned).

\section{The Power Failure and Its Diagnosis}
\label{sec:power}

With relevance defined the obvious way --- a post-$T$ task is
\emph{answerable} iff a metadata-linked prior PR exists strictly before
$T$ --- the harness came back underpowered
(Table~\ref{tab:underpowered}).

\begin{table}[h]
  \caption{Answerable post-$T$ tasks under the metadata-linked relevance
  label, against a pre-registered power floor of $\ge 80$.}
  \label{tab:underpowered}
  \begin{tabular}{rrr}
    \toprule
    Cutoff $T$ (days back) & Post-$T$ tasks w/ issue & Answerable \\
    \midrule
    365 & 278 & \textbf{5} \\
    455 & 306 & 4 \\
    545 & 345 & 4 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Five is not a measurement; it is noise. The intellectually cheap move is to
pivot the evaluation to the known-item number that already looks good and
never mention the failure. We did not, because $n{=}5$ has two
\emph{opposite} explanations with opposite consequences:

\begin{itemize}
  \item[\textbf{A.}] \emph{The label is too strict.} Metadata-linked
    relevance misses precedents transferred by convention rather than by an
    explicit issue link. Then the instrument is rescuable with a wider,
    leak-free relevance definition.
  \item[\textbf{B.}] \emph{Precedents are genuinely rare.} $n \approx 5$ is
    real, and the honest output is a negative result: forward coverage is
    structurally small.
\end{itemize}

One cannot tell A from B by staring at the number. We drew 30 random
post-$T(365)$ tasks and hand-checked, independently of metadata links,
whether a genuine relevant precedent existed strictly before $T$. A genuine
pre-$T$ precedent existed for at least 14 of 30 under a strict reading, and
up to roughly 29 of 30 under a lenient one: the metadata label undercounted
true relevance by roughly \textbf{14--29$\times$}. Verdict: \textbf{A}. The
first number was not the finding --- it was a symptom, and the probe
identified the disease.

\section{Rebuilding Relevance: TREC Pooling with Human Calibration}
\label{sec:pooling}

If metadata links undercount relevance, relevance must be judged, not
inferred. Judging every pair is infeasible, so we pool: for each task, the
top candidates of three independent retrieval legs are unioned into a
judgment pool.

\paragraph{Pool composition.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{eval} --- the evaluated production embedding retriever;
  \item \texttt{bm25\_unique} --- candidates only lexical
    BM25~\cite{robertson2009bm25} (over enriched text) surfaces;
  \item \texttt{bge\_unique} --- candidates only a second-family embedder
    (bge-m3~\cite{chen2024bge}) surfaces.
\end{itemize}
Each leg is included because each contributes relevant precedents the
others miss; concretely, a BM25-only pool recalled just 72\% of
judged-relevant candidates. Independence lives in the judgment, not in the
pool --- the pool is deliberately a union.

\paragraph{Blind gold set.}
We hand-labeled 270 (task, candidate) pairs --- 30 tasks $\times$ 9
candidates, three from each leg --- \emph{before} looking at any depth
result, yes/no for genuine relevance. Density: 65 yes / 205 no (24\%
positive). Coverage resolves the power failure: \textbf{29 of 30} tasks are
answerable under judged relevance (versus 5 under the metadata label),
confirming verdict A by hand.

\begin{table}[h]
  \caption{Per-leg precision on the blind gold set.}
  \label{tab:legs}
  \begin{tabular}{lrr}
    \toprule
    Leg & relevant / total & precision \\
    \midrule
    \texttt{eval} & 46 / 90 & 0.51 \\
    \texttt{bge\_unique} & 11 / 90 & 0.12 \\
    \texttt{bm25\_unique} & 8 / 90 & 0.09 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The production leg is precise --- every second candidate is relevant. The
unique legs are low-precision but decisively non-zero: together they
contribute 19 of 65 relevant precedents (29\%) that the main leg missed ---
the classical pooling trade of coverage bought at the cost of
precision~\cite{zobel1998reliable}. Neither the lexical nor the
second-family leg can be dropped without biasing the pool.

\section{A Negative Result: The LLM Judge Fails Its Gate}
\label{sec:judge}

Hand-labeling 30 tasks rescued statistical power, but the instrument needs
80+ tasks to clear its pre-registered floor, and hand-labeling roughly 720
pairs per corpus does not scale. The plan was an LLM judge to label the
full pool --- \emph{conditional} on passing a pre-registered authority
gate: Cohen's $\kappa$ (judge vs.\ human) $\ge 0.6$~\cite{cohen1960}.

It did not pass (Table~\ref{tab:judge}).

\begin{table}[h]
  \caption{LLM-judge calibration against human gold labels. The
  pre-registered authority gate was $\kappa \ge 0.6$.}
  \label{tab:judge}
  \begin{tabular}{lrl}
    \toprule
    Judge (framing) & $\kappa$ vs.\ human & Verdict \\
    \midrule
    Sonnet 4.6 (phase-9 gold) & 0.27 & below floor \\
    Opus 4.8 (phase-9 gold) & 0.39 & below floor \\
    Sonnet 4.6 (temporal gold) & \textbf{0.39} & \textbf{below floor} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

On the temporal gold set the confusion matrix (human $\times$ judge) is
TP = 23, FN = 42, FP = 7, TN = 198 --- precision 0.77, recall
\textbf{0.35}. The judge misses 65\% of human-relevant precedents, and the
misses concentrate exactly where it hurts: 10 of 11 \texttt{bge\_unique}
and 6 of 8 \texttt{bm25\_unique} relevant precedents --- the semantic and
convention-transfer cases the pool was widened to capture. The judge's
blind spot is aligned with the pool's reason for existing.

\paragraph{Decision.}
The judge is retained \textbf{advisory-only}: its yes-labels are reasonably
trustworthy (precision 0.77); its no-labels are not (recall 0.35), and it
can never override the recall guardrail. We prefer reporting a slower,
human-anchored result over laundering a coverage number through a labeler
we have measured to be unreliable on the cases that matter. Consequently,
the full-power forward-coverage number remains \emph{open} and
human-labeling-bound; we track it as such rather than substituting a
pretend value.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Design guidance.}
Four practices did disproportionate work in this study. (1)
\emph{Pre-register the gates}: the $\kappa \ge 0.6$ authority floor and the
$\ge 80$-task power floor were fixed before any result existed, so a
disappointing result could not be redefined into a passing one. (2)
\emph{Blind-label before looking}: the gold set was labeled before any
depth result was inspected, so labels could not be rationalized toward a
desired outcome. (3) \emph{Treat the first number as a symptom}: $n{=}5$
was not the answer; the probe that explained it was. (4) \emph{Report
negative results as results}: a judge that fails calibration and a coverage
question that remains open are stated plainly, with the numbers.

\paragraph{Threats to validity.}
Everything here is validated on a single corpus (\texttt{dotnet/efcore});
the pipeline's connector seam supports further sources, but no second
corpus is evaluated yet, and efcore's disciplined issue-linking culture
likely makes our metadata-label finding \emph{conservative} for messier
repositories. The production retriever uses a single embedding family;
the pool mitigates but cannot eliminate family bias. Judge prompting was
varied across two framings but not exhaustively engineered; a materially
better judge may yet pass the gate, which is precisely why the gate is
pre-registered rather than abandoned. The corpus is English-language.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Precedent retrieval over merged-PR history is easy to demonstrate and hard
to measure. A frozen known-item instrument shows strong ranking quality
(recall@5 = 0.90 on deduplicated \texttt{dotnet/efcore}); a leak-free
temporal-holdout instrument for the harder forward-coverage question
required rebuilding the relevance definition itself --- from metadata links
(underpowered by 14--29$\times$) to TREC-pooled, blind human-calibrated
judgments --- and exposed a measured failure of LLM-as-judge labeling
($\kappa = 0.39 < 0.6$) concentrated on the cases that matter most. The
instrument stands; the scalable labeler does not, yet. We release the
system, the evaluation harness, and the hash-pinned manifests, and we
report the open questions as open.
