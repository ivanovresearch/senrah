\begin{abstract}
Retrieval tools for AI coding agents are trivial to demo and hard to
measure. We study the evaluation of \emph{precedent retrieval} --- ranked
semantic search over a repository's merged pull-request history, where each
item is a problem--solution pair --- and show that the standard known-item
protocol, while necessary, systematically overstates practical utility. On a
backport-deduplicated corpus of 575 merged PRs from \texttt{dotnet/efcore},
known-item retrieval achieves recall@5 = 0.90 (MRR@10 = 0.79, 218 held-out
queries), yet this measures only ranking quality. We construct a leak-free
temporal-holdout instrument for the harder question --- forward coverage:
whether a genuinely new task retrieves a relevant precedent that strictly
predates it. A metadata-linked relevance label left the instrument
underpowered (5 answerable tasks against a pre-registered floor of 80); a
30-task labeled probe attributed the failure to the label, not the corpus,
undercounting true relevance by 14--29$\times$. We rebuild relevance with
TREC-style pooling over three retrieval legs and a blind human gold set of
270 pairs (24\% positive; 29/30 tasks answerable). An LLM judge intended to
scale the labeling failed its pre-registered calibration gate (Cohen's
$\kappa$ = 0.39 vs.\ a 0.6 floor; recall 0.35 against human labels,
concentrated exactly on the semantic-transfer cases the pool exists to
capture) and was retained as advisory-only. We report this negative result
as a first-class finding and derive design guidance for evaluating
history-grounded retrieval without laundering coverage numbers through
uncalibrated judges.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{mining software repositories, retrieval evaluation, AI coding
agents, temporal holdout, TREC pooling, LLM-as-judge calibration}
